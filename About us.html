OUR HISTORY

Ever since Rhythm House, the 67-year-old music and video store in Mumbai’s Kala Ghoda district, announced in November that it would soon be winding up business, there has been an outpouring of nostalgia and regret from not just the store’s regular visitors, but many Mumbai-based musicians, film personalities, writers and romantics. The closure of a store that sold music and film in physical form and allowed customers to sift leisurely through its collection has been taken to be symbolic of the end of an age. Also, with other Kala Ghoda landmarks such as Samovar café and Wayside Inn having shut down recently, there is fear that one of Mumbai’s most interesting neighbourhoods is losing its cultural heritage. Rhythm House itself was famous for having something for everyone: from Indian classical music to the entire collections of popular rock bands. Five well-known figures in Mumbai’s music and film world recount their favourite memories of Rhythm House.

More than two years ago, on 14 June 2013 to be precise, I received the last music and movies chart compiled by Rhythm House in my inbox. The staff compiled fortnightly charts based on CD and DVD sales. That fortnight, Grammy-winning British vocalist Adele’s brilliant second album, 21, had just scaled up eight positions from No.10 and reached No.2. The No.1 spot belonged to a compilation of songs by 2013’s Grammy nominees. I have been on the store’s mailing list for over a decade, and these charts gave me a sense of what the micro-minority that still bought CDs at one store in Mumbai was listening to. It was also strangely comforting to know that people bought music at a physical store. So the mail announcing that the charts would be discontinued only reaffirmed what I already knew: CD sales had fallen too hard and too low to be able to do justice to their charts. In a badly lit corner behind the cash counter, I found an album by Swarn Noora, a singer from Punjab, whose voice led me to one of the most exciting journeys I have ever been on as a music journalist. I tracked down the website Beatofindia.com, which had released Noora’s album, and travelled with its team across Madhya Pradesh to listen to some of the best folk balladeers in the country. If I had not chanced upon the album at Rhythm House, I wouldn’t have ever written that story.
